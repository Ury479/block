# ✅ Part B — Concept Explanation

### **1. Explain the difference between Business Ethics and Social Responsibility.**

Answer: Business Ethics is the influence of company  while Social Responsibility is the service of company to feed back the society.

Correct answer: Business Ethics = the moral principles/standards that guide decisions and behavior in business; Social Responsibility (CSR) = the organization’s responsibility to consider and manage its impacts on society and the environment (often beyond legal compliance and profit).  
Result: ❌

Knowledge points being tested:

- Definition-level distinction: “Ethics = how you decide/behave”; “Social responsibility = what obligations/impacts you take responsibility for.”
    
- Scope: Ethics often focuses on individual + organizational decision-making (right vs wrong). CSR focuses on broader stakeholder and societal impact (people/planet/profit).
    
- Relationship: A company can be ethical but still do minimal CSR, and a company can do CSR activities but still have ethical problems (e.g., donate a lot but engage in corruption).
    

Detailed explanation:

- Business Ethics answers: “Is this decision/behavior right or wrong?” Examples: honesty in advertising, avoiding bribery, fair treatment, data privacy, conflicts of interest.
    
- Social Responsibility answers: “What should the company contribute or change to reduce harm and create social value?” Examples: reducing carbon footprint, responsible sourcing, employee wellbeing programs, community investment, accessibility initiatives.
    
- Key correction to your wording:
    
    - “Influence of company” is too vague and not the core idea.
        
    - CSR is not just “service/feedback to society” (donations). It includes managing negative externalities (pollution, labor practices, data misuse) and long-term sustainability.
        

A simple contrast example:

- Ethics issue: A salesperson lies about product capabilities to close a deal (unethical).
    
- CSR issue: The company designs packaging that creates unnecessary waste and then changes packaging to reduce environmental impact (CSR)

### **2. Explain what Hybrid Software Distribution means and provide one real-world example.**

Answer:Hybrid Software Distribution is the safety work for prototype.

Correct answer: Hybrid Software Distribution = delivering/operating software using a mix of distribution/deployment models, typically combining on-premise (installed locally) and cloud/SaaS components (hosted by provider), often to balance control/compliance with scalability and convenience.  
Result: ❌

Knowledge points being tested:

- “Distribution model” meaning: how software is delivered to users/customers and where it runs/gets operated (on-prem vs cloud).
    
- “Hybrid” meaning: mixing models (not security testing, not prototyping).
    
- Ability to provide a concrete example and justify why it’s hybrid.
    

Detailed explanation:

- On-Premise: software installed and run on the customer’s own servers/infrastructure; customer controls environment and many operational tasks.
    
- Cloud/SaaS: software hosted by the vendor/provider; users access via internet; provider manages infrastructure, updates, scaling.
    
- Hybrid Distribution: part of the system runs on the customer side (on-prem) while other parts/services run in the cloud, or the same product supports both modes together during a transition/migration.

### **4. What does “Business means creation of utility” imply?**

Answer:creation of utilty is the service of company to feed back the society.

Correct answer: It implies that the purpose of business is to create value/benefit (utility) for customers/stakeholders by meeting needs, solving problems, or improving efficiency/experience—not merely producing goods or earning profit.  
Result: ❌

What was being tested:

- “Utility” meaning in business: usefulness/value to customers (form, time, place, possession utility).
    
- Distinguishing value creation from CSR/charity.
    
- Understanding that profit is typically a result of value creation, not the definition of utility.
    

Detailed explanation:

- “Creation of utility” = making something more useful to someone.
    
    - Form utility: turning inputs into a useful product (e.g., software that automates accounting).
        
    - Time utility: available when needed (24/7 service).
        
    - Place utility: accessible where needed (cloud access).
        
    - Possession utility: easy to obtain/use (subscription, app store).
        
- This is not “feedback to society” (that is closer to CSR). A business can create utility simply by solving a customer problem efficiently.
    
How to fix (minimum exam-acceptable):  
“It means businesses exist to create value (usefulness) by satisfying needs—e.g., improving efficiency, reducing cost, increasing convenience.”

### **5. Why do some companies choose B2B instead of B2C?**

Answer:B2B is better than B2C

Correct answer: Companies may choose B2B due to strategic trade-offs such as higher contract value, clearer monetization, longer-term relationships, and easier targeting of defined customers, even though sales cycles are longer and requirements can be stricter.  
Result: ❌

What was being tested:

- Ability to state 2–4 concrete reasons (not a value judgment).
    
- Awareness of trade-offs: B2B advantages and downsides.
    

Detailed explanation (good reasons to choose B2B):

- Higher average revenue per customer (enterprise contracts).
    
- More stable/recurring revenue via long-term agreements.
    
- Clearer customer needs and willingness to pay for ROI (cost saving, compliance, productivity).
    
- Lower need for mass marketing; sales focuses on specific organizations.  
    Common trade-offs (also acceptable to mention):
    
- Longer sales cycle, procurement and negotiation.
    
- Higher requirements: security, integration, compliance, support/SLA.
    
How to fix (minimum exam-acceptable):  
“Some companies choose B2B because contracts are higher value and more stable, customers pay for measurable ROI, and long-term relationships reduce churn—though sales cycles are longer and requirements stricter.”
